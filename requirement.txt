Chapter 1 Introduction
In recent years, artificial intelligence has undergone a profound transformation driven by advances in generative modeling. Diffusion models, originally developed for high-fidelity image synthesis, have demonstrated an exceptional ability to represent complex, high- dimensional probability distributions with remarkable stability and expressiveness. Their underlying principle — iteratively transforming noise into structured data — has proven particularly powerful in domains characterized by uncertainty, multimodality, and com- plex dependencies. As research increasingly moves from digital media generation toward physically grounded applications, diffusion models are beginning to reshape robotics and autonomous systems. Autonomous driving, in particular, represents one of the most challenging and impactful domains in which these generative principles can be applied. Traffic environments are inherently stochastic and interactive. At any given moment, multiple plausible future behaviors exist for each participant. A vehicle approaching an intersection may accelerate, decelerate, change lanes, or yield. A pedestrian may con- tinue walking, stop, or alter direction. Traditional deterministic trajectory models often collapse this uncertainty into a single most-likely forecast, thereby losing critical informa- tion about alternative outcomes. Even probabilistic approaches frequently approximate multimodality using discrete hypotheses or simplified distributional assumptions, limiting their ability to capture rich interaction dynamics. Diffusion models offer a fundamen- tally different perspective by learning the full distribution of possible trajectories rather than predicting a single path. Through iterative denoising, they generate diverse and realistic motion patterns that remain coherent across multiple interacting agents. In this sense, diffusion-based motion models function not merely as predictors, but as proba- bilistic world models capable of synthesizing complex traffic futures. However, prediction alone does not constitute decision-making. Conventional autonomous driving architec- tures separate motion prediction and planning into independent modules, often leading to structural limitations. Predictions are typically open-loop and do not account for how the ego vehicle’s planned behavior might influence others. Planning, in turn, assumes fixed forecasts and may therefore be brittle when unexpected behaviors arise. Further-
 
more, multimodal uncertainty is often reduced to a small set of candidate trajectories, restricting the planner’s ability to reason over diverse futures. These limitations moti- vate the search for more unified frameworks. This thesis explores such a framework by transforming diffusion-based trajectory prediction into controllable generative planning through structured conditioning mechanisms. By embedding goal-related signals directly into the diffusion denoising process, the model can generate trajectories that are not only plausible under learned traffic dynamics but also aligned with specified objectives. Instead of treating planning as a separate optimization problem, it becomes a guided generative process. Conditioning signals may encode maneuver intentions, directional commands, or target states, allowing the system to bias trajectory samples toward de- sired behaviors while maintaining multimodal richness. This formulation bridges the gap between prediction and planning within a single probabilistic architecture. A central contribution of this work is the extension of this approach to multi-agent motion with agent-specific conditioning. Real-world traffic consists of interdependent agents, each pursuing individual goals while reacting to others. Many conditional generative models apply a single global conditioning signal across the entire scene, which limits behavioral granularity. In contrast, the proposed framework introduces separate conditioning vec- tors for individual agents. This modular design enables fine-grained behavioral control while preserving joint interaction consistency. Each agent’s trajectory can be guided independently, yet all motions are generated jointly within a shared diffusion process, ensuring coherence and collision awareness. The result is a controllable multi-agent mo- tion model capable of representing heterogeneous intentions within realistic interactive environments. The proposed approach is trained and evaluated on the nuPlan dataset, comprising over 1,200 hours of real-world urban driving data collected in cities such as Singapore, Pittsburgh, Boston, and Las Vegas. The diversity of traffic patterns and road geometries within nuPlan provides a rigorous testbed for assessing multimodal trajectory generation and conditional planning performance. Experimental results demonstrate that diffusion-based modeling supports realistic and diverse motion generation, effective condi- tional guidance, and structured multi-agent interaction under heterogeneous constraints. By integrating generative modeling, conditional control, and multi-agent coordination, this work advances the transition from passive trajectory forecasting toward controllable, interaction-aware planning. MotionDiffusion represents a step toward autonomous sys- tems that reason probabilistically, generate diverse yet structured behaviors, and operate robustly within complex real-world traffic environments.

Structure of the work. Chapter 2 introduces deep learning, generative models and motion forecasting, plus domain generalization and OT. Chapter 3 details the network architecture and gap-quantification methodology. Chapter 4 presents results; Chapter 5.4 and Chapter 5.4 discuss, limit, and conclude.

Background and Related Work

Generative models have revolutionised machine learning, and their adoption in autonomous driving is advancing prediction and planning. This chapter lays the groundwork for Mo- tionDiffusion, a diffusion-based framework for controllable multi-agent trajectory predic- tion and planning. We begin with neural network fundamentals: the biological neuron analogy, the perceptron and multilayer networks, and how deep learning models are trained with losses, backpropagation and optimisers (SGD/Adam).	Next, we survey sequence modelling (RNNs, LSTM, GRU, attention/Transformers) and why such mod- els naturally suit vehicle trajectory data and interactions. We then review generative model families (VAEs, GANs, autoregressive models, normalising flows), summarising their strengths and weaknesses for multi-modal motion forecasting.
The core of the chapter is diffusion models. We explain the forward noising pro- cess and reverse denoising (including DDPM equations below), training objectives (the ELBO-derived DDPM loss) and the score/SDE perspective. We cover conditional diffu- sion: how to inject context (e.g. goals or commands) via concatenation, FiLM or cross- attention, and how guidance mechanisms (classifier-based and classifier-free) steer the generation. Sampling speed-ups (DDIM, DPM-Solver) are also discussed. Throughout, we relate these topics to motion prediction and planning: why diffusion’s ability to repre- sent diverse futures and to accept conditioning makes it well-suited to MotionDiffusion, especially when each agent’s behavior can be guided independently. This architectural shift enables coherent multi-agent sampling under separate controls. Finally, we list ex- plicit assumptions (e.g. using the nuPlan dataset, Transformer encoder, UNet denoiser) and sketch a recommended chapter structure.

2.2	Sequence Models for Trajectory Prediction
Trajectories constitute inherently temporal data, as each position depends on previous motion states. Sequence modelling techniques are therefore a natural choice for trajectory prediction tasks.
 
Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) were introduced to process sequential inputs by maintaining a hidden state that evolves over time. At time step t, the hidden state is updated according to
ht = f (ht−1, xt),	(2.4)
where xt denotes the input at time t, ht−1 is the previous hidden state, and f (·) is a nonlinear transformation. Through this recursive formulation, the network output at time t depends implicitly on all past inputs {x1, . . . , xt}. This property makes RNNs well-suited for modelling time series and motion trajectories.
However, standard (“vanilla”) RNNs suffer from the vanishing and exploding gradient problem when trained on long sequences, which limits their ability to capture long-range dependencies (Hochreiter). To address this limitation, Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks (Hochreiter and Schmidhuber) were introduced. LSTMs have demon- strated strong performance in domains such as speech recognition, machine translation, and video classification (Smagulova and James). Their innovation lies in a gating mech- anism that controls the flow of information into and out of a memory cell, including the input, forget, and output gates. These gates allow the network to retain or discard information as needed, stabilizing gradient propagation over long sequences.

Figure 2.2: Recurrent Neural Network, Long Short-Term Memory and Gated Recurrent Units architectures (Olah, 2015).

In autonomous driving, LSTMs have been widely used for trajectory forecasting, as they are capable of capturing motion context over several seconds. Notably, models such as Social-LSTM incorporate social pooling mechanisms to account for interactions between neighbouring agents, enabling the prediction of socially compliant motion pat- terns. Yet traffic scenes involve many interacting agents and complex context (road graphs, traffic rules). Attention mechanisms, which we going to learn more about at the next chapter, address this by letting the model focus on relevant parts of the input. The Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) uses self-attention to relate every part of the input to every other part. In multi-agent motion forecasting, this means an agent’s fu- ture can attend to the histories of nearby vehicles and map features. Indeed, recent work (e.g. Wayformer) successfully applies Transformers to trajectory prediction, encoding all
 
agent trajectories and map data as a set of tokens. This flexibility makes Transformers well-suited for our purposes: they naturally produce per-agent context vectors that can be used in a diffusion decoder.
In summary, sequence models (RNN/LSTM) provide temporal processing, while at- tention/Transformers enable rich interaction modelling. MotionDiffusion uses a Trans- former encoder to extract agent-specific features from the scene, preparing the ground for conditioned diffusion sampling.

2.3	Generative Models
Early approaches to trajectory prediction predominantly relied on recurrent neural net- works (RNNs) and sequence-to-sequence architectures. A common formulation is the encoder–recurrent–decoder (ERD) framework, originally proposed by Alahi et al. ?. In this architecture, an encoder processes an observed trajectory sequence into a fixed- dimensional latent representation, which initializes a recurrent decoder that autoregres- sively generates future states.
Formally, let x1:Tobs denote the observed trajectory. An encoder network fϕ maps this sequence into a latent state h = fϕ(x1:Tobs ). A decoder RNN then predicts future positions as
xt+1 = gθ(xt, h),	t ≥ Tobs.
This paradigm has been widely adopted due to its simplicity and compatibility with variable-length sequences. Numerous extensions incorporate social pooling mechanisms, interaction graphs, or handcrafted features to account for neighboring agents.
However, ERD-style models suffer from a fundamental limitation: they are determin- istic. Training typically minimizes a mean squared error (MSE) loss between predicted and ground-truth trajectories, which implicitly assumes a unimodal conditional distribu- tion. As a result, these models tend to predict an average of all plausible futures, leading to unrealistic behaviors such as trajectories drifting between lanes or cutting across in- tersections. This phenomenon, often referred to as mode averaging, makes deterministic sequence models unsuitable for realistic motion forecasting in complex traffic scenes.

Autoencoders
Autoencoders represent one of the earliest neural architectures for unsupervised represen- tation learning. Introduced by Rumelhart et al. Rumelhart et al. [1986b], an autoencoder consists of an encoder network fϕ and a decoder network gθ. The encoder maps an input x ∈ Rd to a lower-dimensional latent representation z ∈ Rk, where k < d, and the decoder reconstructs the input from this latent code.

The key contribution of autoencoders is the demonstration that neural networks can learn compact latent representations that capture salient structure in high-dimensional data. Such representations have proven useful for dimensionality reduction, denoising, and feature extraction.
However, autoencoders are fundamentally deterministic. The latent representation produced by the encoder does not follow a prescribed distribution, and there is no prin- cipled way to sample new data points from the latent space. As a result, classical au- toencoders do not constitute generative models in a probabilistic sense. While they can reconstruct inputs and perform denoising, they cannot generate diverse novel samples, which limits their applicability to tasks requiring uncertainty modeling.

Variational Autoencoders
Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) introduce a probabilistic latent-variable formulation that enables principled generative modeling and sampling from a learned data distribu- tion. The framework was independently proposed by Kingma and Welling Kingma and Welling [2014] and Rezende et al. Rezende et al. [2014] and represents a foundational approach to stochastic neural generation.
A VAE assumes that observed data x is generated from an unobserved latent variable z, drawn from a prior distribution p(z), typically a standard multivariate normal distribu- tion p(z) = N (0, I). The generative process is modeled through a likelihood distribution
pθ(x | z), parameterized by a decoder network. Since the true posterior p(z | x) is gener-
ally intractable, it is approximated by a variational distribution qϕ(z | x), parameterized by an encoder network.
Training is performed by maximizing the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO) on the marginal log-likelihood log pθ(x), given by
LVAE = Eqϕ(z|x)[log pθ(x | z)] − KL qϕ(z | x)   p(z) ,

where the first term encourages accurate reconstruction of the input, while the second term is the Kullback–Leibler (KL) divergence that regularizes the latent space by enforc- ing similarity to the prior distribution.
In practice, the encoder network outputs the parameters of a Gaussian distribution in latent space, namely a mean µϕ(x) and standard deviation σϕ(x). To enable gradient-

Despite these advantages, VAEs exhibit notable limitations in high-dimensional and multimodal settings. The Gaussian likelihood assumption and the averaging behavior induced by reconstruction losses often result in overly smooth or blurred samples. In trajectory prediction, this manifests as conservative motion forecasts that fail to capture sharp maneuvers or distinct behavioral modes, such as aggressive lane changes or late braking. Consequently, while VAEs provide a principled probabilistic framework, they are insufficient for modeling the rich multimodal distributions required for realistic multi- agent motion forecasting.

Generative Adversarial Networks
Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), introduced by Goodfellow et al. Goodfellow et al. [2014], constitute a major class of generative models that learn a target data dis- tribution through an adversarial training process. In contrast to latent-variable models
 
 
Figure 2.4: Architecture of a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN). The generator G maps random noise z to synthetic samples, while the discriminator D attempts to distinguish generated samples from real data Dendorfer et al. [2023].

such as VAEs, GANs do not rely on an explicit likelihood function. Instead, they frame generation as a two-player game between two neural networks with opposing objectives.
A GAN consists of two components:
•	a generator G(z), which maps random noise z ∼ p(z) (typically N (0, I)) to syn- thetic samples in data space, and
•	a discriminator D(x), which outputs the probability that a given sample originates from the true data distribution rather than from the generator.
An overview of the GAN architecture is shown in Figure 2.4, illustrating the adver- sarial interaction between the generator and discriminator.
Training a GAN is formulated as a minimax optimization problem:

min max V (G, D) = Ex∼pdata [log D(x)] + Ez∼p(z)[log(1 − D(G(z)))].

Here, pdata(x) denotes the real data distribution and p(z) is a simple prior distribution over latent noise. The generator seeks to minimize this objective by producing samples that are indistinguishable from real data, while the discriminator seeks to maximize it by correctly classifying real and generated samples.
At the global optimum of this game, the generator perfectly replicates the data dis- tribution, i.e., pg = pdata, and the discriminator becomes maximally uncertain, yielding
D∗(x) = 1 .
2
Under this condition, the GAN objective corresponds to minimizing the Jensen–Shannon divergence between the generated and true data distributions, and the system reaches a
 
Nash equilibrium.
GANs have demonstrated impressive performance in high-dimensional data genera- tion, particularly in image synthesis, where they are capable of producing sharp and photorealistic samples. Numerous architectural variants, such as DCGANs and Style- GANs, have further improved sample quality and visual fidelity.
Despite these strengths, GANs suffer from significant practical limitations. Training is often unstable and highly sensitive to hyperparameter choices and network capacity balancing. A common failure mode is mode collapse, where the generator learns to pro- duce only a limited subset of the data distribution, ignoring other valid modes. In the context of trajectory prediction, this behavior is particularly problematic, as it suppresses behavioral diversity and may fail to represent rare but critical maneuvers.
Furthermore, GANs do not provide an explicit probability density or likelihood es- timate, which complicates quantitative evaluation and principled conditional sampling. These limitations make adversarial approaches difficult to deploy reliably for structured, multimodal prediction tasks such as multi-agent motion forecasting, motivating the con- sideration of alternative generative frameworks.

2.4	Diffusion Model Theory
This section presents the mathematical foundations of diffusion models, including Denois- ing Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) Ho et al. [2020] and score-based stochastic differential equation models (score-SDE) Song et al. [2021]. The exposition is tailored to motion trajectory modelling.
We first introduce the forward noising process and derive its closed-form marginal dis- tribution. Subsequently, we formulate the learned reverse process and show how the stan- dard DDPM training objective arises from a variational evidence lower bound (ELBO). We then relate diffusion training to denoising score matching and continuous-time SDE formulations.
Conditional sampling mechanisms are derived, including classifier guidance and classifier- free guidance (CFG). We provide the interpolation formula between conditional and un- conditional models and present standard sampling algorithms such as the DDPM sampler, DDIM, and the ODE-based view underlying DPM-Solver.
Throughout this section, the clean trajectory is denoted by

x0 ≡ X ∈ RN ×T ×d,

representing N agents over T timesteps with state dimension d. For mathematical conve- nience, we flatten X into a vector in RD with D = NTd. Each agent i may be associated with a conditioning vector ci, e.g., features extracted by a Transformer encoder.

Continuous-Time Diffusion (Score-SDE View)
Apart from the discrete-time (Markov chain) formulation of diffusion models, one can describe diffusion in continuous time via stochastic differential equations (SDEs). In this view, the forward noising process is defined by an Itô SDE of the form

dx = f (x, t) dt + g(t) dwt,

where wt is a standard Wiener process, f (x, t) is a drift term, and g(t) controls the noise strength. This equation continuously injects Gaussian noise into the data. Under common parameterizations (e.g. Variance Exploding/Variance Preserving SDEs), the marginal p(xt | x0) is Gaussian, typically written as
p(xt | x0) = N xt; s(t)x0, σ2(t)I ,

where s(t) and σ(t) are deterministic functions of time. As t grows, s(t) decays and σ(t) grows so that xt approaches an isotropic Gaussian, analogous to the discrete-time case where xT is nearly pure noise.
Reversing this process yields a reverse-time SDE. By Anderson’s theorem (and sub- sequent work by Song et al.), the dynamics of the reverse diffusion are given by
dx = f (x, t) − g(t)2∇x log pt(x) dt + g(t) dw¯t,
where w¯t is a Wiener process evolving backward in time, and ∇x log pt(x) is the score function of the marginal at time t. Since the true score is unknown, a neural network sθ(x, t) is trained to approximate it. Equivalently, one can train a network to predict the original clean data x0 from a noisy sample xt by minimizing squared error; this yields the same objective as score matching. In other words, the model learns to map xt to the estimated score or to the denoised sample, enabling sampling of the reverse SDE.
Importantly, Song et al. also showed that there exists a deterministic probability flow ordinary differential equation (ODE) with the same marginal distributions as the SDE. This ODE is
dx = f (x, t) − 1 g(t)2∇ log p (x).
Integrating this ODE from t = T down to t = 0 (starting from xT ∼ N (0, σ2	I))
deterministically transforms noise into a data sample. In practice, numerical solvers (such as higher-order Runge–Kutta or Heun’s method) are used for stability and accuracy.
In summary, the continuous-time SDE framework is equivalent to the discrete-time diffusion up to time discretization. We focused on the Markov chain model in this thesis, but the SDE/ODE viewpoint provides an alternative interpretation. It confirms that the same generative process can be seen either as a chain of Gaussian transitions or as
 
solving a continuous stochastic (or deterministic) differential equation, reinforcing the probabilistic foundations of diffusion models.

2.5	Conditional Diffusion and Guidance Techniques
Modern diffusion models extend beyond unconditional data generation by allowing con- trol over the semantic or contextual attributes of the generated samples. In the context of motion forecasting, this conditioning mechanism is crucial: the goal is not to sample arbi- trary trajectories from the marginal distribution p(x) but rather to model the conditional distribution p(x | c), where c encodes the observed past trajectories, the road geometry, traffic signals, and interactions with other agents. This section provides the theoretical foundation of conditional diffusion processes and the associated guidance mechanisms that enable controllable generation.

Although the conditional diffusion model theoretically learns p(x | c), in practice one often wishes to further amplify the influence of the condition during sampling. This
process, known as guidance, controls the trade-off between the realism and the adherence to the conditioning signal.

Classifier Guidance
Dhariwal and Nichol [2021] introduced classifier guidance, which uses a separately trained conditional classifier pcls(c | xt). During sampling, we compute the classifier’s gradient with respect to the noisy input and add it to the model’s score. Formally, the guided score is approximated as

∇xt log p(xt | c) ≈ ∇xt log p(xt) + w ∇xt log pcls(c | xt),

where w > 0 is a guidance weight. Larger values of w drive the samples to satisfy the condition more strictly, at the cost of reduced diversity or possible artifacts. This method gives fine-grained control over conditioning, but requires training an auxiliary classifier on noisy inputs at every diffusion timestep, which can be computationally expensive and limits conditioning to predefined classes.

Classifier-Free Guidance
Ho and Salimans [2022] proposed classifier-free guidance (CFG) to avoid a separate clas- sifier. The diffusion model is trained to predict noise both conditionally and uncondition- ally by randomly dropping the condition during training (replacing c with a null token
∅ with probability pdrop). The network thus learns to output

ϵθ(xt, t, c)	and	ϵθ(xt, t, ∅),

where ϵθ is the noise-prediction model. At inference time, these two predictions are combined to adjust conditioning strength:
ϵCFG = ϵθ(xt, t, ∅) + γ ϵθ(xt, t, c) − ϵθ(xt, t, ∅) ,
where γ ≥ 1 is the guidance scale. When γ = 1, this reduces to standard conditional sampling. For γ > 1, the model emphasizes the conditional prediction over the uncon- ditional one. Intuitively, this multiplies the conditional score by (1 + γ) and subtracts γ times the unconditional score. Larger γ values increase fidelity to c but can cause the samples to collapse toward a narrower mode of the conditional distribution.
Classifier-free guidance has become the de facto standard in conditional generation
 
(e.g. text-to-image and trajectory forecasting) because it provides an effective mechanism to tune the influence of the condition without extra model components. In practice one chooses γ by validation (e.g. γ ≈ 2 often works well), balancing adherence to c against sample diversity.

Practical Considerations and Trade-offs
•	Guidance scale γ: Higher values yield samples more strongly matching c (higher fidelity) but tend to reduce diversity, may introduce artifacts and collapse modes. Lower values allow broader variation but weaker adherence.
•	Computational cost: CFG requires two model passes per sampling step (condi- tional + unconditional), roughly doubling cost.
•	Diversity vs. fidelity trade-off. In tasks like multi-agent trajectory generation, choosing γ is critical: too high → all agents behave similarly (collapsed modes), too low → poor conditioning.
•	Negative prompts and anti-conditioning. One can also compute ϵθ(xt, t, cneg)
for an unwanted condition and subtract it in guiding to avoid certain attributes.

Extensions and Advanced Guidance
Recent research has sought to refine guidance techniques further:

•	Time-step or adaptive guidance: Guidance strength γt varies across timesteps (e.g., weaker early, stronger late) to maintain diversity early and fidelity later Adaloglou and Others [2024].
•	Training-free guidance: Methods that induce guidance at inference time with- out special training overhead (“Independent Condition Guidance”, “Time-step Guid- ance”) Sadat and Others [2024].
•	Manifold-constrained CFG (CFG++): Introduces constraints to keep sam- pling closer to the data manifold, reducing mode collapse and improving invertibility Chung et al. [2024].

2.6	Forecasting-to-Planning via Controllable Genera- tion
Autonomous driving means anticipating how other drivers might act and translating that anticipation into safe, realistic maneuvers. This tightly links motion forecasting (how the
 
scene may evolve) with motion planning and control (how the ego vehicle should act in response). Unlike supervised learning tasks with clear labels, future trajectories are complex, uncertain, and strongly context-dependent: given the same history and map layout, multiple outcomes can be plausible at once (e.g., yielding vs. merging, turning vs. going straight). In this chapter, we therefore study approaches that connect prediction to planning and control by treating forecasting as conditional generative modeling: instead of outputting a single “best” trajectory, we aim to model a distribution over possible fu- tures that enables uncertainty-aware and risk-sensitive decisions. A particularly suitable framework for this is MotionDiffuser [Jiang et al., 2023], which uses conditional diffusion models to represent joint multi-agent future distributions and, crucially, supports con- trollable sampling via guidance—making it a natural bridge from trajectory prediction to motion planning.

In conclusion, physics-based approaches have been shown to provide efficient and ex- plainable predictions. However, their hand-crafted dynamics and simplified assumptions limit their ability to model highly interactive, long-horizon behaviour.

Reinforcement-learning-based methods These approaches cast trajectory predic- tion as a sequential decision-making problem, modeling agents as goal-directed entities that act to optimize future outcomes given the current state and long-term intent. Agent motion is typically governed by an explicit cost/reward structure capturing preferences and constraints such as comfort, safety, energy efficiency, and compliance with traffic rules.
A common subclass comprises planning-based, goal-informed predictors: given a start state and a target location, an agent is assumed to follow an optimal path that minimizes a hand-crafted cost (e.g., smoothness, obstacle proximity, and scene semantics). Yi et al. (2016) construct an energy map by representing the scene layout, static objects, and dynamic agents as energy fields and combining them into a unified landscape; optimal
 
trajectories are then computed using the Fast Marching algorithm. Karasev et al. (2016) extend this line of work by formulating prediction as a Jump Markov Decision Process, where the agent’s goal is treated as a latent, slowly varying variable inferred from recent behavior; a stochastic policy then generates action sequences that maximize expected cumulative reward while accounting for uncertainty and nonlinear dynamics. González et al. (2016) model driver behavior with an MDP over discrete states and learn the under- lying cost parameters via Inverse Reinforcement Learning from expert demonstrations, enabling the model to capture nuanced behaviors such as risk aversion and interaction patterns. In follow-up work, González et al. (2017) incorporate Dynamic Bayesian Net- works to better represent multi-agent dependencies over time.
Despite their interpretability, RL-based methods are limited by strong rationality assumptions, i.e. that agents act (near-)optimally with respect to an internal cost. In essence, human behaviour can be defined as bounded rational, or inconsistent, due to the impact of distractions and external constraints. These factors have the capacity to compromise performance in real-world settings. Nevertheless, it presents preliminary methodologies for multiagent control, which are utilised in this paper.

Attention-based models Nayakanti et al. (2023) discuss how attention mechanisms can fuse multiple modalities in motion forecasting (e.g., agent history, roadgraph context, and interactions). They distinguish three common fusion strategies:
•	Late fusion: Each modality is encoded separately (often with attention-based en- coders), producing modality-specific representations that are then combined (typ- ically via flattening and concatenation) before downstream processing or the final prediction head.
•	Early fusion: Modalities are projected into a shared embedding space early, and a single global attention module operates on the combined tokens/features, enabling cross-modal interactions from the start.
•	Hierarchical fusion: Modalities first undergo intra-modal attention indepen- dently; the resulting representations are then merged and passed through an addi- tional attention stage in a shared space to explicitly model cross-modal interactions.
The best strategy depends on the task, modality structure, and compute budget; empir- ically, Nayakanti et al. (2023) report that early-fusion approaches scale most efficiently with the number of model parameters.

Other generative approaches: VAEs and GANs Before turning to diffusion mod- els, we briefly review alternative generative families that have been widely used for multi- modal trajectory prediction. Conditional VAEs (CVAEs) generate a distribution over
 
future trajectories conditioned on the past motion and context (e.g., interactions or scene cues). For example, DESIRE [?] combines a CVAE-style trajectory sampler with recur- rent refinement that scores and adjusts candidates over time. A common limitation of CVAEs, however, is the use of a simple (often standard Gaussian) latent prior, which can mismatch the true latent structure of complex behaviors and introduce modeling bias, especially for strongly multi-modal futures.
Several works address this by enriching the latent structure or the regularization. ? (or similar CVAE interaction-focused setups) encode interactions via spatiotemporal graphs and employ discrete categorical latents to represent high-level behavior modes, improving interpretability and prior alignment. ? propose Wasserstein autoencoder variants with attention mechanisms and Wasserstein-based regularizers to better match aggregated posteriors and priors. Going further, ? introduce conditional normalizing flows inside the VAE (CF-VAE), yielding more expressive conditional priors/posteriors that can cap- ture complex, multi-modal trajectory distributions, particularly when visual context is available.
GAN-based approaches provide an alternative route to diversity. ? propose an LSTM encoder–decoder generator with social pooling to model interactions and a discriminator that distinguishes plausible from implausible trajectories. Subsequent methods (e.g., SoPhie [?], MATF-GAN [?]) integrate map context (often via CNN features) and social interaction cues, while modifying discriminator designs to evaluate trajectory realism more effectively. Despite strong sample diversity, GANs are often harder to train reliably and can suffer from instabilities such as mode collapse [?].

Focus of this thesis: planning and control via conditional generation In this thesis, we are particularly interested in planning- and control-oriented forecasting: rather than generating futures that are merely likely, we want to steer predictions toward out- comes that satisfy explicit objectives or constraints (e.g., reaching a goal, respecting waypoints, avoiding collisions, or expressing domain-specific behavior). This naturally calls for generative models that can be conditioned on rich context and, crucially, allow controllable sampling at inference time. In the following, we therefore emphasize diffu- sion models, since conditioning and guidance mechanisms provide a principled handle for steering trajectory generation toward desired plans.

Why conditioning helps. n diffusion-based trajectory generation, conditioning plays a crucial role in improving both the realism and controllability of the produced futures. An unconditioned diffusion model must represent all possible maneuvers within a single score field, including going straight, turning left, turning right, slowing down, or per- forming a lane change. Because these behaviors can be fundamentally different from one
 
another, the model is implicitly forced to average across them. In complex road environ- ments, this often leads to trajectories that blend multiple modes, resulting in unrealistic behavior such as drifting forward when a turn is intended, producing overly smooth curves that do not match any real maneuver, or failing to align with the structure of the road map. By contrast, a conditioned diffusion model learns a set of mode-specific score fields that are indexed by a behavioral control variable, which explicitly indicates the intended maneuver. This allows the model to specialize its internal representation and denoising dynamics for a particular type of motion. For example, when the condition specifies a left turn, the model can focus on generating trajectories with sharper curvature, ap- propriate changes in orientation, and alignment with left-turn lanes in the map, rather than compromising between multiple possible directions. As a result, mode fidelity is significantly improved and the tendency toward ambiguous, average behavior is reduced. In the context of autonomous driving, this distinction is especially important because future vehicle motion is inherently multimodal and cannot be uniquely determined from past observations alone. At an intersection, for instance, the same past trajectory might reasonably lead to several different futures, depending on the driver’s intention or route choice. Without conditioning, the model has no mechanism to resolve this ambiguity and is forced to produce a compromised prediction. Conditioning provides a direct way to express high-level intent and therefore makes it possible to generate trajectories that are consistent with a specified goal or maneuver. This not only increases realism, but also en- ables controllable sampling of future paths, which is highly valuable for downstream tasks such as motion planning, scenario analysis, and safety evaluation in autonomous vehicle systems. By explicitly incorporating behavioral intent into the generative process, the model moves beyond passive prediction and becomes an active, controllable component within a broader decision-making pipeline.

Several practical design choices proved to be crucial for stable training and realistic trajectory generation in our diffusion-based motion forecasting framework. A first important aspect concerns the consistent use of masking throughout the entire pipeline. All attention operations as well as the loss computation explicitly respect the provided masks in order to avoid any leakage from padded or invalid entries. Since the number of agents and the available trajectory length vary across scenes, unmasked attention would allow the model to incorporate artificial patterns from padding regions, which would negatively affect both training stability and prediction quality.  By strictly enforcing these masks in all modules, the network is forced to learn only from physically meaningful and valid data, which significantly improves robustness and generalization to unseen scenes.
Another key factor influencing performance is the choice and consistency of the dif- fusion noise schedule. In diffusion-based models, the variance schedule σ(t) determines
 
the magnitude of the injected noise and therefore directly affects the difficulty of the denoising task at each timestep. In our experiments, we observed that even small mis- matches between the schedule used during training and the one applied during inference can lead to unstable behavior and noticeable degradation in trajectory quality. For this reason, we carefully ensured that the same σ-schedule is applied in both phases. This strict alignment stabilizes the optimization process, leads to smoother convergence, and improves the model’s ability to generalize during sampling, as it is always evaluated under conditions consistent with its training distribution.
The location at which conditioning information is injected into the network also plays a decisive role in the overall performance. In our approach, the explicit behavioral control signal—derived from the future motion direction of the ego agent (e.g., straight, left turn, or right turn)—is first projected into the latent space and added to the trajectory embeddings on the future segment. This feature-level conditioning alone already provides improved controllability and helps the model to align its predictions with the intended maneuver. However, in complex environments characterized by dense interactions and intricate road topologies, we found that an additional integration of the control signal at the attention level further enhances robustness. By conditioning the attention mechanism itself, the model can reinterpret spatial and temporal relationships among agents and map elements under the specified behavioral intent, which is particularly beneficial in scenarios such as intersections, lane changes, and merging events.
To further improve flexibility and increase the diversity of generated trajectories, we optionally employ classifier-free guidance (CFG). During training, the conditioning signal is randomly dropped with a small probability puncond, which in our implementation is defined by the parameter P_UNCOND. This enables the network to learn both conditional and unconditional generation within a single model. During inference, the two modes are combined according to
ϵ˜ = (1 + w) ϵcond − w ϵuncond,
where ϵcond and ϵuncond denote the conditional and unconditional noise predictions, re- spectively. The scalar w corresponds to the guidance strength (cond_scale in our code). Lower values of w encourage higher diversity by allowing the model to explore multiple plausible futures, whereas higher values enforce a stronger adherence to the specified behavioral condition and lead to more deterministic, goal-aligned trajectories.
In summary, our method extends standard trajectory diffusion models by introduc- ing an explicit behavioral control variable that is consistently incorporated at both the embedding and attention levels. The entire system is trained end-to-end using a pre- conditioned denoising objective, ensuring numerical stability across different noise scales. At inference time, classifier-free guidance allows for controllable yet diverse trajectory generation while maintaining strong consistency with the underlying road geometry. Due
 
to its modular design and the use of standard deep learning components, the proposed framework integrates seamlessly into the existing codebase and is fully compatible with efficient mixed-precision training on clustered GPU systems.

3.4	Data Preprocessing
The preprocessing pipeline (as implemented in map_preprocessing.py) converts raw sce- nario XML files into fixed-size, ego-centric tensors suitable for a multi-agent diffusion model. It begins by loading and parsing CommonRoad-formatted scenario files (here derived from nuPlan), then filters and transforms agent data into an ego-centric frame, extracts relevant road geometry (lanelet centerlines), and finally aligns trajectories to a uniform temporal grid with normalization. Each step is carefully implemented in code, ensuring that only valid agents are kept and that all outputs have consistent shape and scale. The result is a structured dataset of segments, each containing observed/predicted trajectories, map polylines, masks, and normalization parameters, ready for training or inference in a graph-based model.

Scenario Loading and Parsing The pipeline reads each scenario’s CommonRoad XML file using xml.etree.ElementTree. It first extracts road geometry by finding every

3.5.2	Conditional Architecture: High-Level Intent Control
The unconditional model architecture described in the previous section (3.5.1) successfully learns a realistic motion prior from the nuPlan dataset, but it cannot be controlled to achieve specific tactical goals. When driving a car without a driver, a system must be able to predict what might happen in the future and respond to explicit instructions like "turn left" or "maintain lane". This section is about the conditional extension of the architecture. This is designed to change the generative process into a controllable planning tool through high-level intent signals.
 
The main problem in creating conditional trajectories is making sure that a single command can effectively influence the whole prediction sequence, which is often high- dimensional. Unlike local features, such as speed or nearby obstacles, a maneuver inten- tion is a ’global’ property. This means it defines the meaning of the path from start to finish. To do this, we use a multi-stage system that extracts, projects, and integrates labels into the diffusion denoising process.

Geometric Intent Extraction. To avoid the cost and noise of manual labeling, high- level intent labels are extracted automatically from the ground-truth future trajectories during training. The system relies on a geometric heuristic based on the cumulative change in heading over the prediction horizon.

Based on a threshold of 15◦ which had the best results from 10 − 25◦, each trajectory

The class is represented as a one-hot vector y ∈ {0, 1}3, which serves as the conditioning signal.

Condition Projection Network The discrete maneuver command must be mapped into the continuous latent space of the model before it can influence trajectory generation. For this purpose, a dedicated projection network CondProj is introduced.
The projection network consists of a small multilayer perceptron (MLP) with SiLU
activations that transforms the one-hot command vector into a dense embedding:

econd = CondProj(y) ∈ R256	(3.27)
The resulting 256-dimensional embedding matches the internal feature dimension used by the Transformer denoiser.
Two variants of the projection network were implemented:

•	CondProjShort: a two-layer MLP

•	CondProjLong: a deeper four-layer MLP

The deeper variant was found to provide more expressive representations and is used in the final architecture.
 
Temporal Expansion of the Condition Signal Unlike map features or interaction signals, the intention of the manoeuvre is a global property of the future trajectory, rather than a local feature at a single timestep. So, the condition embedding must have an effect on all the future states that are predicted. To achieve this, the predicted state of the system is sent out along the prediction horizon:

Econd = expand(econd) ∈ RA×Tpred×256	(3.28)
where A denotes the number of agents and Tpred the prediction horizon. The condi- tioning signal is only used for the future part of the sequence, while the past part stays the same. This design keeps the same order of events in the prediction problem. The past is a fixed context, and the future is still controllable.

Feature-Level Conditioning The first integration point of the condition signal occurs at the feature level before the Transformer backbone. After the trajectory features are embedded and compressed through the feature encoder, the expanded condition tensor is added to the latent representation of the future tokens:

xt = xt + Econd,	for t ≥ Tobs	(3.29)
This additive fusion injects the intention of the manoeuvre direct into the internal representation of the predicted trajectory segment. However, simple additive condition- ing alone can not be enough as we have seen during the testing. This is because the transformer layers may gradually weaken the conditioning signal as deeper layers process the features. To address this limitation, a stronger conditioning mechanism is introduced.

Feature Modulation via FiLM Conditioning To reinforce the influence of the ma- neuver command throughout the network, feature-wise linear modulation (FiLM) is ap- plied before each Transformer block. Given the latent trajectory representation x and the condition embedding Econd, a small linear projection generates scaling and shifting parameters:

(γ, β) = Wfilm(Econd)	(3.30)
The latent features are then modulated according to

x′ = x ⊙ (1 + γ) + β	(3.31)
where ⊙ denotes element-wise multiplication.
This operation allows the maneuver condition to dynamically reshape the internal feature representation by selectively amplifying or suppressing specific feature dimensions.
 
Compared to purely additive conditioning, FiLM modulation provides a significantly stronger mechanism for controlling the generated trajectories.
Importantly, the modulation is applied before every Transformer block, ensuring that the conditioning signal remains present throughout the entire denoising process.

Attention-Level Conditioning As well as conditioning at the feature level, the Trans- former also has an attention mechanism that includes the maneuver embedding. The condition embedding changes the query representation used to calculate attention in the cross-agent attention layers and the agent cross-roadgraph layers:

Q′ = Q + WcondEcond	(3.32)
This change makes the attention mechanism focus more on contextual features that match the commanded manoeuvre. For example, when the model is told to turn left, it can focus on map elements related to left-turn lanes or intersection shapes. Also, when an agent wants to turn left, they need to see the other agent in front of them. This strat- egy of combining two different types of conditioning – feature modulation and attention conditioning – allows the maneuver signal to influence both the internal representation and the interaction reasoning performed by the Transformer..

Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG). To ensure the conditioning signal is robust and to allow the strength of the guidance to be tuned during inference, the model is trained using Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG). This technique eliminates the need for a separate classi- fier to guide the generation, instead training a single model to act as both a conditional and an unconditional predictor.
Training phase (conditioning dropout). During training, the conditioning signal y is randomly dropped with probability puncond (set to 0.1). When dropped, y is replaced by a learned null token (or a zero vector). This forces the model to learn two distributions simultaneously: the conditional distribution pθ(x | c) and the marginal (unconditional)
distribution pθ(x).

toward the specified intent. This enables explicit control over the trade-off between mode coverage and adherence to user commands. In our Experiments we got the best results with s between 1.5 and 2.2.

This result suggests that projection capacity is not the limiting factor. Since the input consists only of a three-dimensional one-hot vector, even the smaller projection network appears sufficient to encode the condition reliably. Consequently, the main design ques- tion is not how to project the condition, but how to inject it into the denoiser. Because CondProjLong performs marginally better and introduces only a modest parameter in- crease, we use it as the default in the remaining experiments.

5.1.2	Conditioning in Attention Layers
With the projection network fixed, we next compare four mechanisms for injecting the condition embedding ec ∈ R256 into the transformer denoiser.

Additive (M1a/M1b).	The condition embedding is directly added to the embedded feature vector at each future time step:
h′ = ht + ec,	∀ t > Tobs.

This is the simplest and cheapest strategy, but it cannot explicitly modulate the attention mechanism.

Query Injection / QInj (M2a–M2c).	In the network_2_ata_atr architecture, the cross-agent attention layer is modified so that the condition influences the query:

Q = Wqh + Wcec.	(5.3)

This allows the commanded direction to affect how each agent attends to neighbouring agents.
 
FiLM (M3a–M3c). Feature-wise Linear Modulation ? conditions the query represen- tation inside the agent–road-graph attention layer. This enables the model to alter how it uses road context depending on the commanded direction.

Token (M4a–M4e).	The networks_2_token architecture introduces a dedicated AgentConditionCr block. Here, the condition embedding is treated as one or more learnable key/value tokens
attended to by the trajectory tokens:

Attn(Qtraj, Kcond, Vcond).	(5.4)

This is the most expressive mechanism because it provides an explicit pathway through which condition information can influence the denoising process. The token architecture also uses 40 transformer blocks instead of 30, making it the deepest model considered.

5.2.1	Effect of Guidance Scale
At inference time, classifier-free guidance (Equation 3.33) amplifies the model’s response to the condition by a scalar factor s. All experiments reported so far use s=3.5. This subsection examines the sensitivity of trajectory quality and directional controllability to the guidance scale.
A higher guidance scale s pushes the predicted trajectory further in the direction indicated by y, at the cost of potentially moving the sample off the learned data manifold. Conversely, s=1 recovers the standard conditional model without guidance amplification, while s=0 yields the unconditional prediction.

and report ADE, FDE, direction accuracy, and separation. Once completed, a table and line plot of ADE vs. direction accuracy as a function of s should be added here.
Based on preliminary observations, we expect a convex ADE curve: low guidance (s < 2) wastes the condition signal, while high guidance (s > 5) amplifies the condition at the expense of physical plausibility. The optimal range is anticipated near s ∈ [3, 4].
